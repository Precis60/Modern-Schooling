Mathematics and Real Life

July 26, 2025 • by Lucas Anderson

I used to be one of those students who constantly asked, "When will I ever use this in real life?" when it came to algebra. Well, today I got my answer, and it completely changed my perspective on mathematics.

Mrs. Chen introduced us to a project where we had to plan a school fundraising event. Sounds simple, right? Wrong! It involved so much math that my head was spinning by the end of class.

The Fundraising Challenge

Our task was to organize a movie night to raise money for new sports equipment. We needed to figure out:

  • How many tickets to sell to break even
  • What price to charge for maximum profit
  • How much popcorn and drinks to buy
  • Whether renting a bigger venue would be worth it
  • How to handle different pricing for students vs. families

At first, our group (me, Emma, Jake, and Priya) thought we could just guess at reasonable numbers. Mrs. Chen quickly showed us why that wouldn't work!

Algebra in Action

This is where algebra became our best friend. We had to create equations for:

  • Revenue: R = (price per ticket × number of tickets) + (snack sales)
  • Fixed Costs: venue rental + movie license + equipment setup
  • Variable Costs: snacks + drinks + promotional materials
  • Total Costs: C = Fixed Costs + (Variable Cost per person × attendance)
  • Profit: P = R - C

The most interesting part was discovering that there's actually an optimal ticket price. Too low, and we don't make enough profit per ticket. Too high, and fewer people buy tickets. We used quadratic equations to find the sweet spot!

The Numbers Game

Here's what our research revealed:

  • School hall rental: $150 (fixed cost)
  • Movie license: $300 (fixed cost)
  • Sound/projection setup: $100 (fixed cost)
  • Snacks per person: $2.50 (variable cost)
  • Promotional materials: $50 (fixed cost)

So our total fixed costs were $600, and we had $2.50 in variable costs per attendee.

Finding the Sweet Spot

We surveyed other students about what they'd be willing to pay:

  • At $5: 200 people said they'd attend
  • At $8: 150 people said they'd attend
  • At $10: 100 people said they'd attend
  • At $12: 75 people said they'd attend

Using this data, we created a demand equation:
Attendance = 250 - 12.5 × Price

Then we could calculate profit for any price:

Profit = (Price - 2.50) × (250 - 12.5 × Price) - 600

The Lightbulb Moment

When I realized that businesses use these exact same calculations every day, everything clicked. Suddenly, algebra wasn't just letters and numbers on a whiteboard - it was a powerful tool for making smart decisions.

Using calculus (which Mrs. Chen briefly showed us), we found that the optimal price was $10.20, which would attract about 123 people and generate a profit of $347.

But we also had to consider practical factors:

  • Round numbers are easier for ticket sales
  • We wanted to keep it affordable for students
  • We needed to account for no-shows (about 10%)

Our Final Decision

Our group decided on $8 tickets (including popcorn) and calculated we'd need to sell 75 tickets to break even. Anything above that would be pure profit for the sports equipment fund.

At $8, we expected about 150 people to buy tickets, but planned for 135 actual attendees (accounting for no-shows). This would give us:

  • Revenue: $8 × 150 = $1,200
  • Costs: $600 + ($2.50 × 135) = $937.50
  • Profit: $262.50

Beyond the Numbers

But the math didn't stop there! We also had to consider:

  • Seating capacity: Making sure we didn't oversell
  • Timing: Analyzing when most students would be available
  • Competition: Checking for other school events
  • Weather backup plans: What if outdoor setup wasn't possible?

Real-World Applications

Mrs. Chen showed us how this project connects to so many careers:

  • Business owners use these calculations for pricing products
  • Event planners optimize attendance and profitability
  • Marketing managers analyze demand curves
  • Restaurant owners calculate food costs and menu pricing
  • App developers determine freemium vs. paid pricing strategies

Technology Integration

We used spreadsheets to model different scenarios and created graphs showing how profit changed with price. Seeing the visual representation made the math concepts so much clearer!

Emma even created a calculator where we could input different variables and instantly see the projected profit. It was like having a business forecasting tool!

Reflection

Math class will never feel the same again. I'm actually looking forward to tomorrow's lesson on linear programming!

The best part? Our movie night proposal was so well-researched that the school actually approved it! We're screening "Spider-Man: Into the Spider-Verse" next Friday, and I'm confident our math will lead to a successful fundraiser.

Who knew that algebra could be so practical and exciting?

What's the most surprising place you've encountered math in real life? I'm curious to hear about other students' experiences with "practical math"!
